FAQ

Is a satellite hotspot the same as a wildfire?

No. A "hotspot" is a single ~375 m or ~1 km pixel that the satellite thinks is anomalously hot for that location. Most hotspots in the Southeast are controlled burns, burn piles, agricultural field fires, or industrial heat sources — not wildfires. A small minority are real uncontrolled fires.

How do you tell the difference?

You can't, with satellite data alone — that's what the local fire department is for. What you can do is look at the cluster pattern, the time of day, and the energy output:

  • Single pixel, low FRP (under 5 MW), middle of the day, daytime confidence "n": probably a burn pile or small controlled burn.
  • Cluster of detections that grow over consecutive satellite passes, FRP rising: something is actually getting bigger — worth checking local news.
  • Same exact lat/lon every time, persistent: almost certainly an industrial heat source (a flare stack, a steel mill, an asphalt plant).

Why are some fires from a few days ago still showing?

The "Trail" dropdown on the timeline controls how many recent hours of detections are visible at any moment. Switch to "5 d" or check "Show all" to see the full 5-day history. Older detections fade from red → orange → yellow → grey so you can tell freshness at a glance.

What's "FRP" and why does it matter?

Fire Radiative Power, in megawatts. It's how much heat energy the satellite measures coming off the hot pixel during that observation. It's the best proxy for "how big is this thing" because pixel temperature can saturate (i.e., a really hot fire and a moderately hot fire can look the same in the brightness reading).

  • < 5 MW: small heat source — burn pile, industrial flare
  • 5–20 MW: small ground fire
  • 20–100 MW: moderate active fire
  • 100+ MW: large/intense fire — watch this one

What does "Confidence: Nominal" mean?

VIIRS classifies detections as Low / Nominal / High. MODIS uses a percentage 0–100. This is the algorithm's own assessment of whether the hotspot is "real" or could be a false positive (sun glint, coastline thermal contrast, cloud edge, etc). Nominal means "likely real, but not certain."

How quickly does the dashboard update?

  • NASA FIRMS: every 30 minutes (and the satellites themselves only pass overhead a few times a day, so detections lag the actual fire start by hours).
  • NWS Red Flag Warnings / Fire Weather Watches: every 15 minutes.
  • The page itself reloads its data every 60 seconds — no refresh needed.

Will it tell me if my house is on fire?

No. Satellite hotspot data lags real-time by hours. For anything actually happening near you, call 911 and trust your local fire department's CodeRED notifications.

Can I get alerts?

Alerting (SMS + email) is on the roadmap and will fire when a detection lands within 25 mi of Hahira, or when a Red Flag Warning is issued for the local NWS zone.

Why was the timeline empty when I first opened it?

If you're checking right after a long quiet period (winter, post-rain), there may genuinely be no recent detections in the area. That's good — go enjoy your day.
